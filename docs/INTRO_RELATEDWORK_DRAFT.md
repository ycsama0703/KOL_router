## 1. Introduction (draft)

**P0 — The agentic trading wave and its social media gap.** LLM-based trading agent frameworks have proliferated rapidly [FinAgent 2024; TradingGPT 2023; MarketSenseAI 2024; FinMem 2023; FINCON 2024; TradExpert 2024], driven by a compelling premise: agents can execute the full intelligence pipeline — data ingestion, feature construction, model building, and order generation — far faster than any human analyst, absorbing information at a scale and speed no individual expert can match. Yet a conspicuous gap persists. A recent survey of LLM trading agents [Yu et al. 2024] finds that *"incorporating social media data is an understudied field"* and that nearly no deployed agent system formally ingests real-time social media streams; the rare exceptions (e.g., TradingAgents [Han et al. 2024]) apply uniform sentiment aggregation without distinguishing *who* originated a narrative or *when* relative to its birth. This is not a deliberate design choice — it reflects the absence of a workable entry point for a data source that is structurally different from institutional news: high-volume, identity-heterogeneous, and acutely time-sensitive.

**P0.1 — The same gap spans event forecasting, where timing is even more decisive.** Social media is not merely a trading input; in *event-prediction* settings — forecasting whether a real-world event will occur, as traded on prediction markets or benchmarked by forecasting systems — a freshly-originated narrative is often the *first observable evidence* that an event is materializing, and the value of that evidence collapses the instant the crowd reacts. Yet the event-prediction literature exhibits the identical blind spot. Social-media event-prediction models either aggregate all posts equally [StockNet 2018; Sawhney 2020], detect *volume spikes after the crowd has already reacted* [first-story detection: Petrović 2010; Tafti 2016], weight by static follower count — which is actively misleading, since a majority of financial influencers are "antiskilled" yet attract *larger* followings [Kakhbod et al. 2025] — or, at best, weight by historical *accuracy* rather than *origination timing* [Zhou 2025]. The one line that does ask "who originated this" — rumor-source detection — performs *retrospective* forensic inference on a *completed* cascade, never as a *forward* routing filter [survey: 2021]. Meanwhile, LLM-based prediction-market forecasters retrieve *news articles, not social streams at all* [Halawi 2024; Schoenegger 2024]. Across both trading and forecasting, an origination-aware, point-in-time routing filter for social streams is a genuine empty cell.

**P1 — Two failure modes of naïve social media ingestion.** When agent systems incorporate social streams without a dedicated filtering stage, two compounding failures arise. (i) *Latency*: the market impact of a market-moving post is compressed into a few seconds — volatility spikes within one second, directional impact lasts on the order of two seconds, and the reaction dissipates to baseline within roughly ten [Scharnowski 2025]; by the time a raw pool is aggregated and routed through an LLM, the window has closed. (ii) *Dilution*: feeding a large candidate pool to an LLM degrades its selection quality rather than improving it. In our experiments, a large LLM handed the full stream achieves a follower-weighted reach-capture of 0.455; the same LLM, given only a cheap pre-filtered shortlist, reaches 0.636 — a 40% relative gain (§4). Opposing signals cancel, the model defaults to generic conclusions, and large volumes of data, compute, and time are wasted. The raw information-acquisition power of agent systems thus actively undermines their usefulness on time-critical social signals.

**P2 — The missing origination gate.** What the pipeline needs is a gate that fires *at the moment of narrative origination* — before any resharing, early-adopter signal, or popularity is observable — ranks incoming narratives by their eventual follower-weighted reach, and delivers only a high-signal shortlist to the expensive downstream model. This is a *point-in-time (PIT) early-ranking* problem under strict latency and token budgets. No existing tool solves it directly: cascade models (DeepCas [Li 2017]; DeepHawkes [Cao 2017]) require an early-adopter observation window; content models (BERT, FinBERT, E5, full LLMs) read post text and are both costly and — as we show — less accurate than our structure-only approach; CasMS [Zhou 2024], the only zero-observation cascade model, still depends on message text and a static adopter-propensity signal.

**P3 — Our discovery.** We show that financial KOL streams contain a stable, identity-specific **lead-lag originator structure**: certain accounts systematically *originate* narratives before others *adopt* them, and this structure is measurable point-in-time from pre-period posting history, deconfounded against posting-hour and timezone. The structure is strong enough that a **simple linear model over it — reading zero text — already outranks every text encoder (NDCG@3 0.745 vs. ≤0.732) and every full LLM tested (DeepSeek 0.623; local 7–8B models 0.53–0.55)**, at ~0.002 ms and 0 tokens per item. Encoding the structure as a directed lead-lag graph and training a listwise cross-entropy GBDT — our **Lead-Lag Router (LLR)** — lifts performance to NDCG@3 0.812 with a statistically significant increment over a strong context baseline (+0.0072 NDCG, pooled, 90% CI). Replacing the real graph with an identity-shuffled control degrades performance by −0.011 NDCG (95% CI), confirming the gain comes from genuine origination-network topology rather than added model capacity.

**P4 — Contributions.**
1. **An originator-structure discovery + verification.** We define a deconfounded, PIT lead-lag originator trait (scalar O_k + graph net-degree g_net) for financial cashtag KOLs and verify it is a real, identity-specific signal via shuffle controls.
2. **A cheap structure router that beats text SOTA.** A linear/tree ranker over the structure (zero text) matches or beats SOTA text encoders and full LLMs on pre-popularity narrative-reach ranking at 2-6 orders of magnitude lower latency/cost.
3. **Rigorous ablations + prior-art comparison.** We attribute the result (listwise objective is the engine; g_net not network-centrality; structure significant only when graph-encoded) and show — by re-implementing them — that prior account signals (Romero influence, Yamada source-spreader, Zhou track-record) are either subsumed by context or add nothing, while our structure adds significantly.
4. **An application.** The router doubles as an early-breakout / event-prediction layer for agentic systems (a budgeted triage stage before expensive LLM reasoning).

**P5 — Roadmap.** Section 2 positions us against prior work; Section 3 defines the data, structure, and router; Section 4 reports the main table; Section 5 the ablations; Section 6 the application.

## 2. Related Work (draft, organized by the audit)

### 2.1 Early / pre-popularity cascade prediction
Cascade-size models such as **DeepCas [Li 2017]** and **DeepHawkes [Cao 2017]** predict future diffusion from an *observed early cascade graph* (random-walk paths or Hawkes intensities over the first hours of adopters). They are accurate but structurally require an observation window and thus cannot fire at origination. **CasMS [Zhou 2024]** is, to our knowledge, the only model with an explicit zero-observation "message-generation" stage; however it relies on message text (BERT) plus an adopter-side retweet-propensity signal over a static social graph. We differ on all of: **zero text**, an **originator (not adopter) lead-lag trait with posting-hour deconfounding**, a **narrative-frame unit with follower-weighted reach** (not single-message retweet count), and a structure-beats-LLM cost claim. A best-effort generation-stage CasMS baseline (its zero-observation inputs — message embedding + originator node2vec graph-position — in our ranker) scores 0.695 NDCG, below text-only (0.719) and far below our structure router (0.813, +0.118); its graph-position embedding alone is weak (0.494). **[Weng 2013]** shows early popularity is a weak predictor and community structure of early adopters helps, but on general memes, requiring early adopters, with no per-account identity trait.

### 2.2 Content-only popularity prediction
**[Stokowiec 2017]** predicts popularity from title text alone with a BiLSTM and argues deep text beats shallow text by ~15%. Our result is the counter-thesis in this domain: a zero-text structural model beats deep text encoders and LLMs. We cite this line as the content-only school our structure router overturns.

### 2.3 Influencers, opinion leaders, and origination traits
A long line measures per-account influence: **[Cha 2010]** (the million-follower fallacy — follower count ≠ influence; influence is stable across topics/time), **[Romero 2011]** (influence-passivity IP score on a directed retweet graph, predictive of URL reach), and recently **[Yamada 2025]** (separating *stable* from *temporal* influencers via a source-spreader-vs-broker split, zero-text GBT). In the financial domain specifically, **[Kakhbod 2025]** finds that a majority of retail "finfluencers" are *antiskilled* yet attract *larger* followings — a direct warning that follower count is not merely uninformative but adversarial as a ranking signal, which is why our trait is derived from lead-lag origination timing and residualized rather than from audience size. These establish that identity-specific, structurally-derived account signals are stable and predictive. We extend this in four ways that prior work does not combine: a **pairwise lead-lag (posting-time) origination** trait rather than self-amplification or eigenvector influence; **residualization against posting-hour/timezone**; a **fresh-narrative reach-ranking** target (not account persistence or URL clicks); and the **financial cashtag** domain. We further re-implement Romero's IP score, Yamada's source-spreader, and a Zhou-style track-record signal on our data and show (Section 5) that they are subsumed by simple context features or add nothing over them, whereas our structure adds a significant increment.

### 2.4 Financial social media
**[Hentschel 2014]** descriptively characterizes cashtag tweets; **[Rakowski 2021]** links aggregate cashtag volume to investor attention and prices via a Twitter-outage natural experiment; **[Li 2025]** predicts abrupt price changes from conversation-level similarity. The closest is **[Zhou 2025]**, which identifies StockTwits experts by *track-record accuracy* to predict *stock returns* and motivates a cheap non-LLM pipeline. We differ on the trait (origination/lead-lag, not accuracy), the target (narrative follower-weighted reach, not returns), the graph (account lead-lag, not stock-stock correlation), and — unlike Zhou, who hedges that the approach "complements, not competes" with LLMs — we run an explicit structure-vs-LLM head-to-head.

### 2.5 Event prediction and prediction markets from social signals
A parallel literature forecasts real-world events from social media, and it exhibits the same origination-agnostic pattern. Content-based movement predictors pool posts equally over a window (**[StockNet, Xu & Cohen 2018]**; **[Sawhney 2020]**); event/burst detectors fire on *volume spikes* — i.e., after the crowd has already reacted — via first-story detection (**[Petrović 2010]**; **[Tafti 2016]**), which is structurally backward-looking. The rumor-source-detection line (**[survey 2021]**) is the only body that explicitly localizes *who originated* a cascade, but it does *retrospective* forensic inference over a *completed* cascade for misinformation containment — never as a forward, point-in-time routing filter, and never for market-moving financial signals. On the forecasting side, recent LLM prediction-market systems (**[Halawi 2024]**; **[Schoenegger 2024]**) retrieve *news articles rather than social streams* and aggregate by LLM-judged relevance. We occupy the empty cell: a *forward*, PIT, originator-first router for live financial social streams. Our event-prediction experiment (§6) instantiates this — ranking freshly-originated narratives by whether they become top-decile reach events, where every text encoder and LLM we test performs near-random while our structure router does not.

### 2.6 Primitives we build on
We detect freshly-originated frames using streaming first-story / novelty detection **[Petrovic 2010]** (here, online cosine clustering of MiniLM embeddings) and a frame representation following **[Qin 2017]**. These are detection primitives, not predictors; they carry no originator signal and are not competitors.

## 3. One-paragraph positioning (for the abstract / intro close)

> Prior work establishes (a) stable, structurally-derived per-account influence/origination traits [Cha 2010; Romero 2011; Yamada 2025], (b) expert identification on financial social media by track-record accuracy [Zhou 2025], (c) zero-observation popularity prediction from message text [Stokowiec 2017; CasMS 2024], and (d) that early popularity is a weak predictor relative to structure [Weng 2013]. We are the first to combine a **deconfounded, point-in-time lead-lag originator structure** over financial cashtag KOLs with a **zero-text lightweight router** that ranks freshly-originated narratives by future follower-weighted reach before popularity is observable — matching or beating SOTA text encoders and full LLMs at orders-of-magnitude lower cost, with the structural signal verified real by shuffle controls and shown to add significantly beyond both strong context features and re-implemented prior account signals.
